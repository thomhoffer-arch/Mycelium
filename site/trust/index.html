privacy · sovereignty · accountability

The four fears, addressed.

Most AEC interoperability stacks ask you to ship project data into someone else's cloud, merge it with a black-box, and trust the result. Mycelium is built the other way around.

1. Your data stays where it is.

Mycelium is a contract, not a hub. Every connector runs inside your environment — your laptop, your server, your VPC — and reads the source it already has access to. There is no central Mycelium server, no mandatory upload, no shared bucket.

The standard is open (Apache-2.0). Your project data is yours. The two never get mixed up because they live in different places.

2. Names stay out of the audit trail (GDPR).

Every write is recorded as a provenance event. Actors in those events are pseudonymous refs — human:u_8f3a, agent:llm, never a name or an email. The mapping from a ref to a real person is kept in a separate, mutable identity store that only the people who need to see it can read.

That separation is deliberate: it lets you honour a GDPR erasure request without breaking the chain of evidence. You delete the mapping; the ledger entries still verify, but the person is no longer identifiable.

3. The audit trail is yours, and it's tamper-evident.

Every proposal, approval and executed change is appended to a log, and each entry carries a SHA-256 hash of the entry before it. Rewrite one event and every subsequent hash stops matching — anyone with the file can detect it.

For a court, an insurer, or a client asking "who decided this, and when?", the answer is a single file you can hand over. No third party, no platform, no subpoena.

4. No vendor lock-in.

Mycelium is an open spec under Apache-2.0. Anyone — including your competitors — can implement it. Connectors live in their own repos under whichever licence their authors choose. The hub indexes them; it does not gatekeep.

If the steward of the project ever drifts in a direction you don't like, you can fork the spec and keep going. That's the point of an open standard: no single party can capture it, including the people who started it.

And the fragmentation problem itself.

The reason AEC data is so often wrong is that "the same thing" lives under a different id in every tool. Mycelium fixes the id problem at the bottom: a stable identity record with multiple keys, a freshness stamp that refuses stale-vs-live joins, and a model-instance guard that refuses to join two copies of "the same" model. Silent wrong answers stop being possible.

What Mycelium is not.

Mycelium does not decide which clash matters. It does not write your reports. It does not run your project. That intelligence — joining, ranking, deciding — is a separate layer that sits on top of the spine. That layer is where the value (and the liability) lives, and it is not part of this open standard.
